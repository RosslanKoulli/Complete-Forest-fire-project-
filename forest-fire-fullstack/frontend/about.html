About this project

A forest-fire ignition prediction and spread simulation system for the Mediterranean climate region, built as a university final-year individual project (CI601, University of Brighton, 2025-2026 academic year).

The system combines three machine-learning classifiers (Random Forest, XGBoost, and a feedforward neural network), trained on two public datasets of historical fires, with a live weather feed and a hexagonal cellular-automaton fire-spread model. The intent is not to compete with operational wildfire-prediction services run by national agencies, but to demonstrate end-to-end how interpretable ML, live meteorology, and physically-grounded fire-behaviour modelling can be assembled into a usable interactive tool.

Scope and limitations

The ML models are trained on roughly 9,800 fire records from the UCI Forest Fires dataset (Portugal), the Algerian Forest Fires dataset, and a Phase 2 extension using ICNF fire records with ERA5 weather reanalysis. All three sources fall inside the Köppen-Geiger Csa, Csb, and Csc temperate dry-summer Mediterranean climate zones.

Predictions outside these zones extrapolate beyond the training distribution. Hexes drawn outside the Csa/Csb/Csc overlay still receive a model output, but a warning is shown in the tooltip and the domain-confidence score is reduced accordingly. This is the project's defensible operational boundary.

How it works

Step 1: Select a region

On the map page, the user draws a rectangle anywhere on the world map. The selection is tessellated into H3 hexagons at the chosen resolution (5 through 8 on the Uber H3 grid; resolution 7 is the default and gives hexes roughly 5 km across). H3 was chosen over a square grid because hexagonal neighbours sit at uniform distance from the cell centre, which simplifies the spread model's wind-direction reasoning.

Step 2: Fetch live weather

For each hex centroid, the system queries the free Open-Meteo forecast API for temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and 24-hour precipitation totals. Hexes whose centroid sits in water (sea-level reported by the DEM) or above 3000m (above the Mediterranean tree-line in all training-region mountains) are flagged as "no fuel" and skipped. The ML models would be extrapolating outside their training distribution for those cells.

Step 3: Compute the FWI components

The weather observations are fed through the Canadian Forest Fire Weather Index (FWI) system, specifically the Van Wagner (1987) recursive equations for FFMC, DMC, DC, and ISI. These four indices describe the moisture content of three fuel layers (fine, duff, deep organic) and the initial rate-of-spread potential. They are the same inputs the trained models received during training, so feature parity is preserved between the static training data and the live runtime feed.

Step 4: Run the three classifiers

Random Forest

Ensemble of 200 decision trees with class-weighting to handle the fire/no-fire imbalance. Strong on nonlinear interactions between FWI components and weather. Trained with SMOTE oversampling.

XGBoost

Gradient-boosted trees with scale_pos_weight set from the empirical class ratio (no oversampling needed). Tends to give the sharpest decision boundary but is more prone to overconfidence at the extremes.

MLP (Neural Net)

Three-layer feedforward network with batch normalisation and dropout. Smoother probability surface than the tree models. Trained with SMOTE.

Each hex receives a probability from all three models. The map can display any one model individually or their arithmetic mean. The mean is the default, since averaging decorrelated classifier errors generally improves calibration compared to picking one model.

Step 5: Reliability scoring

Two reliability signals are computed alongside the probability:

  • Domain confidence: how close the hex's input feature vector is to the training-data distribution, scored 0-100%. Hexes outside the Köppen Csa/Csb/Csc zones, or with weather values far from anything seen during training, drop sharply on this metric.
  • Model agreement: how tightly the three classifiers agree on the same probability. Disagreement among the ensemble is itself a signal that the prediction is fragile.

An overall reliability score combines both. A high probability with low reliability should be read as "the models say this, but I'm not certain they're right here" rather than as a confident high-risk prediction.

Step 6: Cellular-automaton spread simulation

Once predictions are loaded, clicking any hex ignites it and starts a discrete-time cellular-automaton simulation of fire spread. Each step (default 12 simulated hours), burning cells attempt to ignite their six H3 neighbours; the probability of ignition depends on the neighbour's risk score, the wind vector, vegetation moisture, and elapsed burn duration. Frames are streamed from the backend over WebSocket so the spread animates in real time, with the per-frame pacing scaled to the hex resolution (larger hexes animate slower so the apparent spread rate stays physically plausible).

This is a phenomenological model rather than a full fire behaviour simulator like FARSITE or WRF-Fire. Those require digital elevation models, fuel-model maps, atmospheric coupling, and substantial domain expertise to run meaningfully. The cellular automaton trades physical fidelity for interactivity and transparency, so the user can see exactly which cells ignited which neighbours and in what order.

Architecture

  • Backend: Python 3.10+, FastAPI, uvicorn. The three ML models are loaded once at startup from the original project's saved .pkl files. Weather, FWI computation, hex prediction, and the spread simulator are isolated as separate services.
  • Frontend: vanilla JavaScript, no build step, Leaflet for the map, leaflet-draw for rectangle selection, H3-js for the hexagonal grid, Inter and JetBrains Mono for typography. The dark theme is hand-tuned rather than imported from a framework.
  • Data flow: the frontend sends a rectangle and resolution; the backend tessellates, fetches weather, runs FWI and the three classifiers, and streams per-hex predictions over WebSocket so results render as they arrive rather than all at once at the end.
  • Climate overlay: the Csa/Csb/Csc Köppen-Geiger boundary polygons are generated from the Beck et al. (2018) 1 km raster (or Kottek et al. 2006 0.5° as an alternative) by a one-off Python conversion script. The frontend uses these polygons both for visual context and for the point-in-polygon check that drives the in-distribution flag.

What this project does not claim

It does not predict where a fire will ignite. It predicts the model's estimate of the conditional probability that a fire would ignite given the current weather, given training data drawn from one specific climate region. It is a research and educational tool, not an operational forecasting service. Real wildfire response should always be informed by official sources: the national meteorological service, civil-protection authority, and on-the-ground observation.

The spread simulation is similarly indicative rather than predictive. A real fire's path depends on terrain slope, ground fuel continuity, suppression efforts, and small-scale wind that the model does not see.
